Historical Context

The World That Made Marxism

The 19th century was an era of wrenching transformation. Steam-powered machines had replaced centuries of craft tradition, and entire populations were uprooted from rural life into overcrowded industrial cities.

In England — the birthplace of the Industrial Revolution — factory workers toiled 14-hour days, children labored in mines and mills, and wages barely covered survival. The gap between the nouveau-riche industrial bourgeoisie and the masses of working-class poor grew wider with every passing decade.

It was in this crucible of exploitation, inequality, and social upheaval that Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels developed a comprehensive theory explaining — and aiming to end — the suffering they witnessed. They gave the workers of the world not just a diagnosis, but a prescription for revolution.

Political turmoil was also in the air. The failed revolutions of 1848, nationalist upheavals across Europe, and the growing power of organized labor all provided a live context in which Marxist ideas would find fertile ground.

1760–1840

The Industrial Revolution in Britain displaces agrarian communities, creating an urban proletariat working under brutal factory conditions.

1830s

Child labor is commonplace in British factories and mines. The average factory worker earns subsistence wages for 72-hour work weeks.

1848

Revolutionary uprisings sweep through France, Austria, Prussia, and Italy. Known as the "Springtime of Nations," they are largely suppressed but radicalize a generation.

1850s–1860s

The first international workers' associations begin forming across Europe, laying the organizational groundwork for Marxist political action.

The Founders

The Architects of a New World Order

01
Karl Marx
1818 – 1883 · Trier, Prussia

Born to a middle-class Jewish family in the Rhineland, Marx studied philosophy at the University of Berlin, absorbing Hegel's dialectics before turning them on their head. A journalist, economist, and revolutionary theorist, he spent much of his adult life in poverty-stricken exile in London, writing the works that would define modern socialism.

Marx's intellectual output was staggering — from dense economic analyses to biting political pamphlets. His collaboration with Engels gave theoretical weight and empirical grounding to his vision of historical materialism and class struggle.

The Communist Manifesto (1848) Capital Vol. I (1867) The German Ideology Economic & Philosophic Manuscripts
"The philosophers have only interpreted the world in various ways. The point is to change it."
02
Friedrich Engels
1820 – 1895 · Barmen, Prussia

The son of a wealthy textile manufacturer, Engels was no armchair radical. Managing his father's factory in Manchester gave him firsthand exposure to the conditions of industrial workers — documented in his landmark 1845 study of the English working class. Engels was Marx's closest collaborator, intellectual equal, and primary financial supporter.

After Marx's death, Engels edited and published the remaining volumes of Capital, ensuring Marxism's foundational texts reached the world. His ability to translate complex theory into accessible writing made Marxism a movement, not just a doctrine.

The Condition of the Working Class (1845) Anti-Dühring (1878) The Origin of the Family (1884)
"Freedom is the recognition of necessity."
Core Philosophy

The Ideas That Shook the World

Historical Materialism

At the heart of Marxist theory lies a bold claim: that the driving force of history is not ideas, religion, or great men — but the material conditions of economic life. How a society organizes the production and distribution of goods shapes everything else: its laws, its culture, its politics, its religion.

"It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness."

For Marx, history moves through distinct stages — ancient slave society, feudalism, capitalism — each defined by who controls the means of production. Each stage contains internal contradictions that eventually produce revolutionary change, pushing society to the next stage. The final stage, Marx believed, would be communism: a classless, stateless society of free association.

Base

The economic foundation — forces of production (technology, labor, resources) and relations of production (ownership structures, class relations). This determines the superstructure.

Superstructure

The cultural, political, and ideological institutions that emerge from the economic base: law, government, religion, art, philosophy. They reflect and reinforce the interests of the dominant class.

Historical Stages

Primitive Communism → Slave Society → Feudalism → Capitalism → Socialism → Communism. Each transition is driven by contradictions within the existing mode of production.

Class Struggle

Marx opens the Communist Manifesto with one of history's most famous sentences: "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles." Every epoch has been defined by the antagonism between an oppressing class and an oppressed class — master and slave, lord and serf, bourgeoisie and proletariat.

"Workers of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains."

Under capitalism, Marx identified two fundamental classes. The bourgeoisie own the means of production (factories, land, capital). The proletariat own only their labor power, which they must sell to survive. This relationship is inherently exploitative: the bourgeoisie extract wealth from workers' labor while giving back only wages — and they use political and cultural power to maintain this arrangement.

Bourgeoisie

The capitalist class that owns the means of production. They purchase labor power, control production, and accumulate capital. Political power, in a capitalist state, serves bourgeois interests.

Proletariat

The working class that possesses only their labor. They must sell it to survive. As capitalism develops, the proletariat grows larger, more concentrated, and increasingly conscious of its shared interests — becoming a "class for itself."

Class Consciousness

Marx distinguished between a class "in itself" (an objective economic category) and a class "for itself" (one that recognizes its shared interests and acts collectively). Revolution requires the latter.

Surplus Value & Exploitation

Marx's most technically original contribution was his theory of surplus value — an attempt to explain how capitalists profit from workers without engaging in fraud or robbery (in the conventional sense). The answer lay in the unique nature of labor power as a commodity.

"Capital is dead labor, which, vampire-like, lives only by sucking living labor."

Workers are paid a wage equivalent to the cost of reproducing their labor power (food, shelter, etc.). But during their working day, they produce far more value than this. This difference — surplus value — is appropriated by the capitalist as profit. Workers are exploited not through deception, but through the very structure of the wage-labor relationship.

Use Value vs Exchange Value

A commodity has a use value (its practical utility) and an exchange value (what it can be traded for). Labor power is unique: its use creates more value than it costs to purchase.

Necessary vs Surplus Labor

Part of a worker's day covers the cost of their own wages (necessary labor). The remaining time — surplus labor — produces value that flows entirely to the capitalist. This is the source of profit, rent, and interest.

Alienation

Workers under capitalism are alienated from: the product they create, the process of production, other workers, and their own human potential. Work becomes an external compulsion, not a fulfilling expression of human creativity.

Dialectical Materialism

Marx inherited Hegel's dialectical method — the idea that reality develops through the clash of opposing forces — but radically transformed it. Where Hegel saw history as the unfolding of Absolute Spirit (Idea), Marx insisted that the driving contradiction was material: the conflict between productive forces and existing social relations.

"My dialectical method is, in its foundations, not only different from the Hegelian, but exactly opposite to it."

In Marxist dialectics, every historical stage contains within it the seeds of its own destruction. Capitalism creates and concentrates the proletariat, the very class that will overthrow it. Technological advancement eventually outstrips the capacity of capitalist ownership structures to contain it — generating the crisis that propels revolutionary change.

Thesis → Antithesis → Synthesis

A dominant social form (thesis) generates its own opposition (antithesis). Their conflict produces a new, higher synthesis — which then becomes a new thesis, continuing the dialectical process.

Contradiction & Crisis

Capitalism contains fundamental contradictions: between social production and private appropriation; between the drive to lower wages and the need for consumer demand. These periodically erupt as economic crises, each more severe than the last.

Engels' "Three Laws"

Engels systematized dialectics: (1) Unity and conflict of opposites; (2) Transformation of quantity into quality; (3) Negation of the negation. These became the framework for "dialectical materialism" as a comprehensive worldview.

Chronology

A Timeline of Marxism's Rise

1844
Marx Meets Engels in Paris

A meeting in the Café de la Régence begins one of history's most consequential intellectual partnerships. The two men discover their agreement on almost every theoretical question.

1845
Engels Publishes "The Condition of the Working Class"

Based on firsthand observation of Manchester's slums, Engels delivers a devastating empirical account of industrial capitalism's human cost. It provides the raw material for Marx's theoretical work.

February 1848
The Communist Manifesto

Commissioned by the Communist League, Marx and Engels produce a 23-page pamphlet that would become one of history's most influential political documents, calling workers to revolutionary action.

1864
First International Founded

The International Workingmen's Association — the First International — is established in London. Marx drafts its Inaugural Address, bringing Marxist ideas to an organized global workers' movement for the first time.

1867
Capital, Volume I Published

Marx's magnum opus, 15 years in the writing, presents the full theoretical architecture of his critique of political economy. It will eventually be translated into dozens of languages.

1871
The Paris Commune

For 72 days, Paris is governed by a workers' council — the first instance of what Marx called "the political form at last discovered" for proletarian governance. Its violent suppression kills 10,000–30,000 communards.

1889
Second International Founded

Socialist and labor parties from 20 countries form the Second International in Paris, establishing Marxism as the dominant ideology of the organized labor movement across Europe and beyond.

1903
Bolsheviks Emerge in Russia

Lenin's faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party — the Bolsheviks — emerges at the party congress, advocating a disciplined vanguard party to lead revolution. Marxism gains a new, militant form.

October 1917
The Russian Revolution

The Bolsheviks seize power in Petrograd. For the first time in history, a Marxist party holds state power over a major nation. The event electrifies socialist movements worldwide and terrifies ruling classes everywhere.

1919
Communist International (Comintern)

The Third International is established in Moscow, coordinating communist parties across the world and spreading Marxism-Leninism as a global revolutionary movement under Soviet leadership.

Global Reach

How Marxism Spread Across the World

From the factories of Manchester to the paddy fields of Asia, Marxism adapted to local conditions — sometimes barely recognizable in its new forms — but consistently offering a framework for understanding oppression and organizing resistance.

Western Europe

Social Democracy & Labor Movements

In Germany, France, and Britain, Marxist ideas were absorbed by trade unions and socialist parties. The SPD in Germany became the world's largest socialist party by 1912. The tension between revolutionary and reformist Marxism defined European politics for decades.

Russia & Eastern Europe

Bolshevism & Soviet Power

Lenin adapted Marxism to Russian conditions — a largely agrarian society with a small proletariat — by emphasizing the role of a vanguard party. After 1917, Soviet Marxism-Leninism became the dominant variant worldwide, spread through the Comintern.

China & East Asia

Maoism & Peasant Revolution

Mao Zedong fused Marxism with Chinese conditions, centering the peasantry rather than the urban proletariat. After the 1949 revolution, China became the world's most populous Marxist state. Maoism spread to revolutionary movements across Asia and Africa.

Latin America

Revolution & Liberation Theology

The Cuban Revolution (1959) under Castro and Guevara inspired guerrilla movements across the continent. Marxist ideas merged with Catholic liberation theology to produce powerful anti-imperialist movements in Nicaragua, El Salvador, and beyond.

Africa & The Global South

Anti-Colonial Liberation

Marxism provided the ideological framework for anti-colonial struggles across Africa and Asia. Leaders like Kwame Nkrumah, Amilcar Cabral, and Ho Chi Minh blended Marxism with nationalism to mobilize independence movements.

The Academy

Western Marxism & Critical Theory

After WWII, Marxism transformed in Western universities. The Frankfurt School, structuralist Marxism, and later cultural studies reinterpreted Marx through Freud, linguistics, and poststructuralism — making Marxist analysis central to humanistic scholarship.

Legacy

An Unfinished Argument

Marx declared that his goal was not merely to understand capitalism, but to overthrow it. Whether one views Marxism as liberation or catastrophe — or both — its impact on the modern world is undeniable. It gave workers a language and a framework to understand their exploitation. It shaped the welfare state, labor law, and anti-colonial movements even in countries that never became communist.

The 20th century was, in large part, shaped by the contest between capitalism and the various states and movements that claimed Marx's inheritance. That contest produced both extraordinary social achievements and staggering human suffering. Marxism remains alive as a critical tradition — a persistent interrogation of the contradictions of capitalist society.
